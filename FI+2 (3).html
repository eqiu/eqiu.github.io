1.1 Volume Data Wrangling -- Standardizing¶

I further standardize by contract size. The cash all have 1 million dollar notional amounts while the futures have 100k amounts (besides ZT which has 200k). This means that futures should trade 10 times more to equal the same notional amount as cash. So, I divide all future volumes by 10, besides ZT which I divide by 5 to normalize for contract size. This brings some decimals for the futures which is inaccurate, but since I am mainly focused on volume ratios, this point is moot.

I thought about rolling the data, but after weighing the pros and cons of a intraday roll versus a longer term roll, there was no decisive winner, so I have not added rolling yet. The intraday roll would allow me to take advantage of the quick bursts and further standardize to the day. On Brexit for instance, volatility was exceedingly high and if I created an intraday roll it would help me discern the 'true' volume spikes that day. A longer period roll also has merit, because volume is seasonal and will trade more for an extended period of time sometimes. (Perhaps the solution is to create a double roll, one intraday and one longer term.)

1.2 Volume Data Wrangling -- Issues¶

The goal of this project is to find volume relationships between the various products during auctions. The instructions explicitly said to use ratio's; I believe this will be an accurate representation of how a product volume interacts with the other product volumes. This is a very effective standardization technique, but there are a few major issues.

  1. Spurious false data
  2. Missing values
  3. Throwing away a product

Missing values will break the ratios, because it is not possible to divide by nan or 0. In addition, including a ratio has the potential of creating synthetic false data points (imagine using UB which randomly spikes -- then I am not sure if the data point is a larger curve move or if it is just UB). Below I will use a few standardizing techniques to remove these potential issues.

Another possible solution to the two issues raised is to move away from ratios completely and use volume summing. Volume summing just takes sums the volumes at a certain time and divides each product's volume by the total. For instance, if every product traded one time during the hour, the sum would be 11 and the volume summed % would be 1/11 or .091%. Volume summing would still suffer from missing values, but it would be a lot less susceptible to spurious false data and I would not need to throw away a product. The main issue with volume summing is that the data is a little less interpretable. Volume ratios narrow the movement to two pairs, whereas volume summing would give me 11 possible movements.

1.3.1.1 Spurious False Data -- Correlations¶

One way to look for spurious data is correlations; correlations explain relationships between products and if the volume ratio correlations deviate from the volume correlations by too much, there is probably some bogus synthetic data being used (imagine using UB which randomly spikes -- then I am not sure if the data point is a larger curve move or if it is just UB). For example, to make sure a ZN based move is actually a ZN based move I can look at correlations between the original volume data set and my new ratio data set. A correlation matrix will tell me the relationship between all the products and I want those relationships to remain similar when I add in ratio data. Thus, I compute two separate correlation matrices: one with original values and one with the ratios. To measure similarity, I use L1 and L2 matrix distance tests to see which ratio creates the most closest correlations. Suppose matrix $A=(a_{ij})$ and $B=(b_{ij})$, then to get the distances the following equations are used:

\begin{equation} L_1(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n |a_{ij} - b_{ij}| \end{equation}\begin{equation} L_2(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n (a_{ij} - b_{ij})^2} \end{equation}

Thus the smaller the values are the closer to the original volumes and do not have the baseline ratio creating funky data. I have upsampled the volume to one hour which is the minimum time frame analysis I use in my tests.

1.4.2 Multicollinearity¶

Many of the variables from Bloomberg suffer from multi-collinearity. Bloomberg gives me the percentage of primary dealers, the percentage of direct bidders and the percentage of indirect bidders which inherently sum to 1. This means that one product is just a linear transformation of another product and I can find one of the three variables with the other two. This concept is called multicollinearity and is a bid issue in data analysis. Multicollinearity in a regression causes the coefficient estimates to change rather randomly, because the predictor matrix $X$ will not be invertible, due to it not having full rank. This means that an optimal close-form solution with linear regression will not be possible. Thus, there will be many locally optimal solutions that will change rapidly based on what data it is given.

For instance, say a regression is created with two variables that suffer from collinearity X1 and X2 who have 100 data points respectively. A new data point theoretically should not change the coefficients of X1 and X2 that much, but if they are collinear variables adding in a new data point could create a big shift in the coefficients, because the unbiased least squares estimator objective function could change to a different local minimum.

In order to get rid of multi-collinearity in the predictor variables, I delete the direct bidder % column.

2.1 Volume Flows¶

2.1.1 Volume Flows -- Event Outcome¶

A starting hypothesis would be that for each particular auction, that product volume spikes; the 2Y would have more volume with a two-year auction and so on. This would trickle down to the ratios and then '2Y/ZN' ratio should have more volume when there is a two-year auction. In addition, the closest duration matched future should have the same spike, so in the case of a two-year auction 'ZT/ZN' should spike.

When looking at the rest of the curve, I believe that many people are hedging various points of the curve. Fixed income moves are not isolated in a vacuum, if the 2Y yield changes the rest of the curve is affected. I know that certain events affect the front and back end differently and I believe that this is the same case with volume flow. An event that impacts the frontend more, say risk-off terrorist attack, will have more volume flowing into the front end. In the case of auctions, we know exactly which part of the curve will be impacted yield wise and volume (and volume ratios) will follow. A two-year auction should increase the 2Y volume and ZN the most, but should also increase 3Y and ZF a good amount. I would expect a two-year auction to have moderate increases in 30Y and UB, but increase these back-end products the least.

On the flip side, I expect a thirty-year auction to impact the 30Y and UB the most, while not having to much of an effect on ZT and the 2Y.

2.1.2 Volume Flows -- Equation¶

I decide to look at three different time periods: an immediate hour test, a two hour test, and a four-hour test (which goes until the end of the trading day). My goal is to uncover how the various auction variables impact the volume ratios. With this in mind, I set up a multivariate regression with the auction variables (along with dummies indicating what product the auction was) as my X variable and all my volume ratios as my Y variable. My equation then is:

\begin{align*} Y &= constant+a*Coupon+b*Sold+c*Noncompete+d*BidCover+e*HighYield+f*Primary+g*Indirect+h*Two+i*Three+j*Seven+k*Ten+l*Thirty \\ \text{where}~Coupon &= \text{Coupon rate (first difference)} \\ Sold &= \text{Amount sold}\\ Noncompete &= \text{Non-competative bid %}\\ BidCover &= \text{Bid/Cover ratio %}\\ HighYield &= \text{High-yield (first difference)}\\ Primary &= \text{Primary dealer %}\\ Indirect &= \text{Indirect bidder %}\\ Two &= \text{Dummy indicator for two-year auctions}\\ Three &= \text{Dummy indicator for three-year auctions}\\ Seven &= \text{Dummy indicator for seven-year auctions}\\ Ten &= \text{Dummy indicator for ten-year auctions}\\ Thirty &= \text{Dummy indicator for thirty-year auctions}\\ Y &= \text{['2Y/ZN', '3Y/ZN', '5Y/ZN', '7Y/ZN', '10Y/ZN', '30Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', 'ZF/ZN', 'ZB/ZN', 'UB/ZN']}\\ \end{align*}

The dummy column for 5-year bonds is removed to get rid of multicollinearity. In addition, this is a multivariate regression so y is not one number, rather it is the array of my z-scored product ratios. With this regression, I will be able to break down the auction into fragments and see how each small piece interacts with volume flow. Note: I define auction variables as the non-dummy variables in the previous equation.

2.2 Dummy Variables¶

2.2.1 Dummy Variables -- Dummy Variable Trap¶

In my dataset, my last variable is the categorical variable of what which product auctioned. I need to turn the categorical values into ordinal ones to do analysis, but this step needs to be done carefully. Just mapping the variables to values creates more false data. A proper solution would be to create dummy variables indicating which auction it was. In this case, because there are six auctions, I should create six dummy variables. However, I need to throw one of my dummy variables out, or else I fall into the dummy variable trap, which is a subset of the multicollinearity problem plaguing my data earlier: my independent dummy variables would all be collinear. If I know the results of five categories, I inherently know the result of the sixth category. If all the five categories have 0's indicating there was not any auction there, the sixth category has to have a 1. If there is a 1 anywhere in the previous five categories, the sixth category has to be a 0. Thus, the solution is to throw out a variable and the value removed can be thought of as the baseline value.

2.2.2 Dummy Variables -- Interpretation¶

Whatever auction is chosen as my baseline auction will change the outright value of my coefficients, but the estimates of my dependent values will remain constant. Choosing a baseline dummy variable just sets the intercept and relates all choices back to it. If I choose the two-year auction as the baseline, and the intercept for '3Y/ZN' is .3 that means that when there is a two-year auction '3Y/ZN' has a z-score volume of .3; if the coefficient on a three-year auction is .1, it means that when there is a three-year auction '3Y/ZN', there is a .1 increase of z-score volume in relation to the two-year auction. This sums to a total z-score volume of .4 during three-year auction for '3Y/ZN'.

2.2.2 Dummy Variables -- Conclusion¶

I ran a random number generator which gave me the five-year auction as the baseline dummy. I throw out the five-year and leave the two-year, three-year, seven-year, ten-year, and thirty-year auction as dummy variables.

2.3 Hypothesis¶

With my auction variables, I hypothesize that the first differenced variables (coupon and high-yield %) will not have very high predictive powers. Theoretically, the change in coupon and yield should not directly impact volume. (Maybe if there were surprises compared to the when-issued, High-Yield % would be a more predictive variable.) I believe that other variables like Bid/Cover ratio, primary dealer %, and indirect bidder % will have predictive powers. The Bid/Cover ratio tells me how strong an auction was, while the indirect bidder % tells me what percent of the auction was bought up by foreign investors. Foreign investors (mainly central banks) are not trying to offload their notes; they participate in the auction as an investment. On the flip side, primary dealers are generally large banks who will buy the rest of the auction and try to offload them onto the market as soon as possible. When a large percent of primary dealers participates, I expect to see abnormally high volumes.

I touched on the dummy variables briefly, but with the multivariate regression, I can find out how the dummy variables interact with each other. It is expected that a thirty-year auction would be a significant positive predictor in the for 30Y/ZN. However, with my regression results I will be able to compare how the thirty-year auction impacts other products like ZN/ZN and UB/ZN. An auction that impacts the front of the curve should cause those coefficients to be significant. An auction that impacts the back of the curve should likewise cause the coefficients on the back-end dummies to be significant. It would be very shocking/interesting to me if the results of the regressions differ.

Note: In this analysis, I set $\alpha=.05$ and I deem anything below .05 significant.

Auction Coefficients of Auction Variables¶

Many interesting results can be drawn from this regression. In accordance with my hypothesis high-yield % change did not matter for any of the products. There were other variables which were significant for some ratios and not others; these variables include coupon change, bonds sold, and indirect bidder %. Lastly, Bid/Cover ratio, primary dealer %, and noncompetitive % seem to be significant for most of the products. An interesting observation I noticed is that the product ratios on the shorter end of the curve have less significant variables. For instance, for '3Y/ZN' none of the auction variables are significant, but for '30Y/ZN' noncompetitive %, Bid/Cover ratio, primary dealer %, and indirect bidder % are all significant. This hints that the short end volume has more unexplained variation to a auction than the long end volume and is thus harder to predict.

Dummies¶

I can glean more information by looking at the dummy variables. Dummy variables can be thought of as 'what did each product ratio did due to that particular auction over my baseline product.' A good sanity check is that all product ratios associated with their corresponding auctions were significant. We can use the significance of these dummy variables, along with their associated coefficients to draw further conclusions.

Seven-Year¶

In the seven-year auctions, '7Y/ZN' is significant with a positive coefficient, while '5Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', 'ZF/ZN', and 'UB/ZN' are significant with a negative coefficient. This means that during the seven-year auction '7Y/ZN', experiences higher than normal z-scores (compared to baseline five-year auction), while '5Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', 'ZF/ZN', 'UB/ZN' have lower than normal z-scores (compared to baseline five-year auction). Because I am looking at a seven-year auction with a five-year as baseline, I expected '7Y/ZN' to have a positive coefficient while the '5Y/ZN' coefficient is negative. It is also interesting that all the futures have lower volume ratios during this auction. I speculate that this is because the duration matched future for the seven-year is ZN which is my baseline product ratio. Thus, to hedge out the movement in the 7Y, I think people used ZN, which then causes the rest of the future products (that were significant) ZT, ZF, and ZB to have negative product ratio z-scores.

Ten-Year¶

In the ten-year auction, '10Y/ZN' and 'ZB/ZN' are significant with a positive coefficient, while '5Y/ZN', '7Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', and 'ZF/ZN' are significant with a negative coefficient. This result states that during the ten-year auction, 'ZT/ZN' and 'ZB/ZN' experiences higher than normal z-scores ratios that the five-year auction; '5Y/ZN', '7Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', and 'ZF/ZN' receive less than normal z-score ratios. This again leads me to conclude that the closest duration matched products ZN and ZB were used to hedge the 10Y which leads a increase in 'ZB/ZN', while decreasing the 'ZT/ZN' and 'ZF/ZN' ratios.

Thirty-Year¶

The backend auction is in line with the seven and ten-year auctions. During this auction '30Y/ZN' and 'ZB/ZN' had significant positive coefficients while '5Y/ZN', '7Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', and 'ZF/ZN' all have significant negative coefficients. I expected that '30Y/ZN' and 'ZB/ZN' would be positive and significant, but it is surprising to me that 'UB/ZN' is not significant at the $\alpha=.05$ level. The ZB bond is not callable for the first 15 years whereas the UB bond is not callable for the first 25 years. This makes UB a closer duration hedge than ZB and it is very surprising that its volumes do not react to a thirty-year auction over my baseline five-year auction.

Short End¶

Something interesting occurs when looking at the auctions that occur at the short-end of the curve: most product ratios become significant and positive. Looking at the three-year auction '2Y/ZN', '3Y/ZN', '10Y/ZN', '30Y/ZN', 'ZT/ZN', and 'UB/ZN' are all significant and positive while only 'ZF/ZN' is significant and negative. 'ZN/ZF' is negative, because my baseline is the five-year auction which is similar duration wise to ZN. However, the other product ratios are all very interesting. The front-end of the curve and the back-end of the curve all exhibit higher than usual volume ratios. The same pattern holds for the two-year; in fact, there are no products that are significant and negative for the two year. From this data, I conclude that when the short-end has an auction, most of the product ratios along the entire curve spike.

Conclusion¶

This result is for the hour closest to the auction. Bid/Cover ratio, non-competitive percent, and primary dealer % are the most significant variables to determining a product ratio's z-score in the next hour. In addition, the front-end product ratios are harder to predict than the back-end product ratios. When a short-end (two-year and three-year) auction is taking place all product ratios will receive a strong positive boost. When the middle or long-end auctions occur, those product ratios closest in duration will receive a boost and those products ratios further away will have a negative impact. This seems to indicate that during a short-end auction, the entire curve is being hedged, but during other auctions only those closer in duration are being hedged. The only product that breaks this rule is UB as 'UB/ZN' does not spike during the thirty-year auction.

3.1 Cross-Validation¶

I need to make sure my findings hold, especially because I only have around 400 samples. While my results suffer from lack of data, this can be mitigated if cross validation is used to create in-sample and out-of-sample tests. I use a technique called K-folds cross-validation to test for robustness. In K-folds, the entire data set is randomly split into K folds without replacement where, i>k-1</i> folds are used for training and one-fold is used for testing. This method is then repeated K times.

Running K-folds cross-validation has some advantages to more tradition cross-validation techniques like hold-out cross validation, because the method is repeated K times which reduces the individual variance. Typically, with financial data this method is not possible, because it introduces look-ahead bias, but my data are time series naive so it is not an issue.

K-folds cross-validation gives me a good idea of my in-sample and out-of-sample data split. Below I show the in-sample and out-of-sample $R^{2}$ values for my three previous regressions. The index shows how many hours after auction the data is. The first row has a '1' which means its the immedate hour after the auction. The training set for that auction had an $R^{2}$ of .39 while the testing set had an $R^{2}$ of .33. I think its kind of interesting that for all my time periods, the training data set outperforms the testing dataset by around .06.

3.2 Next Steps¶

I took the observation back until 2011, because those were all the tick data that was in g-tick. Given more data, there is no reason why this cannot be taken further. It would be interesting to me to see if these relationships hold up in 2008, or during a period of rising rates like the early 2000's.

I like my regression, because the data is very interpretable. It is possible to boost my $R^{2}$ for out-of-sample testing with principal component analysis, which would map my auction predictor variables to linearly uncorrelated principal components through eigen-value decomposition of the covariance matrix. This would allow me to use only three variables to explain all seven of my auction predictor variables. However, this increase is at a cost to interpretability and the out-of-sample boost is very small.

Other steps could be taken to include the weekly bill auctions and their impact on the notes and bonds. Also, an interesting observation is that on 9/12/2016, both the 3-year and the 10-year auctioned. This potentially could be troubling for my data, but this was the only time they both auctioned at the same time.

If I were to continue this research, I would want to look at the volatility in the market around this time, which could potentially be calculated with bid-ask spreads for each of these products. In addition, getting when-issued data to blacktest all tails and bid-throughs would be ideal.

3.3 Trading Implications¶

As traders, the end goal is always to make money. An interesting relationship that cannot be monitized is not very useful. I believe there are a few ways to use this piece of research to for trading.

First of all, it is important to establish a time period. The research supports the fact that the model I built only holds throughout the first two hours of trading after auctions. However, there is still valuble information to be gleaned in these two hours. When there is a two-year or three-year auction, volume ratios on both the short-end and long-end increase compared to my baseline five-year auction. Market participants trade the whole curve during these short-end auctions and any yield curve strategy should be looking for price discrepancies along the entire curve.

On the flip side, when there is a five-year, seven-year, ten-year, or thirty-year auction, only the closest duration matched products spike and the volume ratio burst is typically contained either to the middle or the back of the curve. In these situations it may be wise to only look for price discrepancies where their are price spikes and not along the entire curve.

Since I do not have price data, I am just speculating on theoretically how the market could move in regards to auctions. It could very well be argued that because there are no volume spikes in front-end during a thirty-year auction, thwere will be a larger price discrepancy between the front-end and the back-end; maybe traders should get into the front-end to mean-revert this spread. However, if a trader does buy into the short-end while the thirty-year auction is takin place, there will be less volume traded compared to rest of the curve, so trades with large size may be hard to get out of. For instance, if one has a steepener, the back-end is liquid and a trader may need to leg out and be unhedged for a bit, because the there are not enough ZT bids/offers at specific price points.

In addition, UB has odd volumes spikes that are not explained by my model. Either stay away from UB or find another explaination for UB's seemingly random spikes.

3.4 Conclusion¶

To conclude, my model will predict z-score ratios which can be used to see which product volumes' increases with regards to my baseline product 'ZN'. Through my analysis, I have found that three variables Bid/Cover ratio, primary dealer %, and non-competitive % are good predictors of product ratios in the immediate two hours. However, just like the dummy variable relationships, they break down after two hours. The model does not suffer too much from in-sample versus out-of-sample testing as my findings consistently hold through multiple time periods.
